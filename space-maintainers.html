Space Maintainers

Baby teeth are supposed to fall out, but sometimes a child may lose a tooth before their adult teeth are ready to grow in. Barad Dental offers a solution to this problem. Our space maintainers are designed to leave enough space open for permanent teeth to grow in after baby teeth are lost prematurely. While it might not seem like a big problem at first, not taking care of a gap can lead to expensive problems down the road.
There are many reasons that lead to premature loss of baby teeth including, trauma, congenital defects, accidents, or decay. Depending on your child’s situation, our experienced dentists will recommend the appropriate type of space maintainers to preserve an everlasting smile for your child.

When do I need them?

Generally children who lose their teeth prematurely are in immense need of space maintainers. We at Barad Dental understand the dire risk of leaving empty spaces to close on their own. The teeth on either side of the gap can shift and close the empty space, which can lead to a serious problem when the adult tooth starts to erupt.
The natural process of losing baby teeth only occurs when the adult teeth are already starting to erupt, as this ensures that the space is only empty for a short period of time, making it impossible for the gap to close.
While space maintainers are rarely recommended for adult teeth, they are sometimes required. We usually recommend space maintainers for adults when teeth are extracted and/or lost. If you feel you need a space maintainer, we are excited to hear from you. So go ahead, contact us directly.

Types of Space Maintainers

As experience dentists, we take into account the well being of our patients, hence employing more than 10 different spacers for each and every child and/or adult. From simple spacers to a plethora of brands and wires, spacers could be difficult to classify. But not for us.
We classify spacers based on their ability to be removed or not (removable & fixed) and their position of the sides of the mouth. Unilateral spacers are fixed on only one side of the mouth while bilateral spacers are fixed on both sides.

Unilateral Spacers

One of the most common fixed unilateral appliances is the band and loop space maintainer. They are positioned on one side of the mouth hence the name unilateral.
Band and loops maintain space after a child loses their primary (baby) first molars or second molars after the permanent first molar erupts around age 6 or 7. The appliance has a band that cements to the primary second molar. It also has a loop that contacts the distal surface of the primary canine.

Bilateral Spacers

The transpalatal arch (TPA) is a bilateral appliance that maintains space after teeth in the upper jaw are missing on both sides.
The TPA contains a thin wire that extends above the roof of the mouth, what we, dentists call the palate, and connects the banded permanent molars on both sides.
The appliance allows for the proper eruption of the premolars in the upper jaw.

Risks of Space Maintainers

One of the main risks of using a space maintainer is oral hygiene. If a child’s oral hygiene is poor, then the gums may become inflamed. In the worst case, inflammation can grow around the space maintainer. Poor oral hygiene can also increase the risk of tooth decay. Young patients may have particular trouble with the process of making and fitting a space maintainer. Certain types of space maintainers, especially the distal shoe, need frequent monitoring and adjusting to ensure the device doesn’t impede the normal eruption of the permanent first molars into the mouth.
